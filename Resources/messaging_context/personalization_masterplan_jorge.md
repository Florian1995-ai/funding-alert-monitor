# Personalization Masterplan for Jorge's Funded Startup Outreach

*Extracted from Florian-Paige Call (Feb 03, 2026) + Jorge Context*

---

## Core Philosophy

> "The more personalized, the better. But there has to be a balance between how much time you spend on the personalization."

> "The first message is maybe 80% of what builds the connection for you to actually book the call. If it's not good enough, they won't respond."

> "Even better than asking them what the problem is: if you already identified it and then present it to them alongside the solution."

## Personalization Techniques Extracted from Paige Call

### 1. SPECIFICITY WITH NUMBERS
**Paige Example:**
> "Out of 117 calls, actually 68 were coming in after hours. I just wondered if you've seen something similar in the industry."

**Jorge Application:**
- "We helped a Series A in Barcelona go from zero devs to first sprint in 14 days"
- "Based on your €5M raise, you're probably looking at 3-4 senior devs to hit next milestones"
- "Most founders I talk to post-raise spend 10-25 hours/week recruiting instead of building"

**Data Points to Include:**
- Funding amount → team size estimate
- Days to first sprint (14 days)
- Time saved (3-6 months → 2 weeks)
- Specific sprint outputs ("shipped auth + onboarding in sprint 1")

---

### 2. GEOGRAPHIC/LOCATION PERSONALIZATION
**Paige Example:**
> "Do you see that in Oxnard or in the LA office? Is this something you're experiencing as well versus the Los Angeles office?"

**Jorge Application:**
- "Is the talent market in Madrid as crazy as Barcelona right now?"
- "I know Berlin has a lot of senior devs, but finding ones who speak Spanish is another story"
- "Are you hiring locally in [city] or open to remote EU talent?"

**Location Angles:**
| Region | Angle |
|--------|-------|
| Madrid | "Capital market, lots of competition for talent" |
| Barcelona | "Startup hub but talent getting expensive" |
| Berlin | "Big tech market, hard to compete on salary" |
| Remote EU | "Timezone alignment, cultural fit" |

---

### 3. NAME-DROPPING COLLEAGUES/PARTNERS
**Paige Example:**
> "Is this something that you and Ashley and Alexandria have discussed at all?"

**Jorge Application:**
- If you find multiple founders: "Have you and [co-founder name] talked about how to scale the dev team?"
- If you find investor names: "I saw [investor name] led the round - they usually push for fast execution"
- If you find advisors: "Is [advisor name] helping with the hiring strategy?"

**How to Find Names:**
1. Press release → founder names, investor names
2. LinkedIn company page → co-founders, CTOs
3. Crunchbase → board members, advisors
4. The Org → org chart

---

### 4. INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC QUESTIONS
**Paige Example:**
> "Do you find the cases that you get for personal injury claims come in after hours?"

**Jorge Application (by vertical):**
| Industry | Question |
|----------|----------|
| AI/ML | "Are you finding it hard to hire senior ML engineers who aren't just prompt engineers?" |
| Fintech | "Is compliance experience a hard requirement, or can you train for that?" |
| DevTools | "Are you looking for devs who've actually used your product in anger?" |
| SaaS | "First eng hire - do you want a full-stack generalist or specialists?" |

---

### 5. DEMONSTRATE THE PROBLEM, DON'T ASK
**Paige Example (Cassette Tape Story):**
> "She called the office after hours, recorded how bad it was... made a recording of that, put the cassette and sent it over to them... You demonstrate to them how bad the problem really is, then what kind of solution you can offer is tremendously powerful."

**Jorge Application:**
- Review their job postings, note how long they've been open
- Check their careers page, see what roles are unfilled
- Look at Glassdoor for "hiring process too slow" complaints
- Check their product for bugs/missing features → implies they need more devs

**Demonstrate, Don't Ask:**
| Instead of Asking | Demonstrate |
|-------------------|-------------|
| "Are you having trouble hiring?" | "I noticed your Senior Backend role has been open 3 months..." |
| "Do you need developers?" | "I saw you're building [feature] - that usually needs 2-3 devs to ship fast" |
| "Is hiring a problem?" | "Most Series A founders spend 15+ hrs/week recruiting. You're probably in that boat too." |

---

### 6. CASE STUDY REFERENCE (The "Chicago Card")
**Paige Example:**
> "We recently completed a project with another Chicago-based law firm. Here's what we found..."

**Jorge Application:**
- "We just helped a similar Series A in [same region] go from zero to first sprint in 14 days"
- "Worked with a [same industry] startup - they shipped their MVP 6 weeks faster than planned"
- "Last month helped a [similar size] raise scale from 3 to 8 devs without the founder touching a resume"

**Case Study Formula:**
```
We helped [similar company type] in [their region] achieve [specific result] in [short timeframe].
```

---

### 7. MULTI-CHANNEL PRESENCE
**Paige Example:**
> "I would also add all of them on Facebook and on Instagram as well. Just so that they see that you have done your research."

**Jorge Application:**
1. **LinkedIn** - Primary (connection request + message)
2. **Twitter/X** - Follow + like recent tweet about the funding
3. **Instagram** (if applicable) - Follow company page
4. **Email** - After LinkedIn connect, or if no response

**Sequence:**
1. Day 1: LinkedIn connect with personalized message
2. Day 1: Follow on Twitter, like their funding announcement tweet
3. Day 2: Email if you have it (reference LinkedIn)
4. Day 5: Follow-up on LinkedIn if no response
5. Day 7: Try different decision maker at same company

---

### 8. CONNECT WITH PRINCIPAL, THEN GO DOWN
**Paige Example:**
> "Now that you've connected with the principal... you can go down and connect with everybody else at the company and send the same message to them."

**Jorge Application:**
1. Connect with CEO/founder first
2. Then message CTO: "I recently connected with [CEO name] - wanted to reach out to you as well since you're probably feeling the hiring pressure more directly"
3. Then message VP Eng: "I've been chatting with [CEO] and [CTO] about scaling the team..."

**Social Proof Cascade:**
```
"I noticed [CEO name] accepted my connect - figured I'd reach out to you too since you're the one who actually has to manage the dev team."
```

---

### 9. ASK FOR PERMISSION
**Paige Example:**
> "Can I share a little bit about that? Sometimes just ask for permission, just having a bit of a back-and-forth conversation."

**Jorge Application:**
- "Mind if I share what we found helping a similar startup?"
- "Can I send over a quick breakdown of what a 14-day sprint looks like?"
- "Would it be helpful if I shared how other founders in your position are solving this?"

**Why This Works:**
- Creates a dialogue, not a pitch
- Gets a "yes" response (micro-commitment)
- Feels consultative, not salesy

---

### 10. BOLD GUESSES (Be Wrong on Purpose)
**Paige Example:**
> "Sometimes it's good to be bold and wrong. And they're like, well, actually, Ashley is not the one that I talked to. But at least you get a response."

**Jorge Application:**
- "I assume you're looking mostly for backend talent given the AI focus?" (might be wrong → they correct you)
- "Guessing the CTO is handling most of the recruiting right now?" (might be wrong → they tell you who is)
- "Sounds like you're probably 3-6 months from needing to double the team?" (might be wrong → they clarify)

**The Point:** A correction is still an engagement.

---

### 11. LET IT SIMMER
**Paige Example:**
> "I'm oftentimes just taking a look at the website, set it aside, let it simmer, maybe go look at that before I go work out. Then I can kind of process that while doing that, go back at that."

**Jorge Application:**
- For high-value leads (€5M+ raises, perfect ICP), don't rush the first message
- Research → close tab → come back later with fresh eyes
- The subconscious will find the angle

---

### 12. DON'T DOUBLE-TEXT
**Paige Example:**
> "Especially with lawyers, like double texting, that's where it really is good to just do volume of leads, because double texting is always bad."

**Jorge Application:**
- If they accept but don't respond → one follow-up only
- If they don't respond to follow-up → move on, try different person at company
- Never send 3+ messages without a response
- Volume > desperation

---

### 13. LINKEDIN VOICE MESSAGES
**Paige Example:**
> "When you actually send on LinkedIn a voice message via your phone, you get like a higher reply rate as well because people are curious."

**Jorge Application:**
- After connect is accepted, send a 15-second voice note
- "Hey [name], just wanted to say congrats on the raise - I work with founders post-funding to get dev teams running fast. Would love to chat if you're still figuring out the hiring side."
- Voice = stands out, feels personal

## Personalization Hierarchy for Jorge

**Tier 1: High-Touch (€10M+ raises, perfect ICP)**
- Full website research
- Find all decision makers (CEO, CTO, VP Eng)
- Personalized message with specific angles
- Multi-channel (LinkedIn + Twitter + Email)
- Voice message follow-up
- Time investment: 15-20 min per company

**Tier 2: Medium-Touch (€2-10M raises, good ICP)**
- Quick website scan
- Personalize with funding amount + region + industry
- LinkedIn only (maybe email)
- Time investment: 5-10 min per company

**Tier 3: Volume (€1-2M raises, okay ICP)**
- Template with merge fields (company, amount, round)
- LinkedIn connect only
- Time investment: 1-2 min per company

Jorge

## Jorge's Tone of Voice (From His Writing Agent)

**Source:** `Resources/Jorgeswriter.md` (Spanish)

### Key Principles (Translated)

| Spanish Original | English | Application |
|------------------|---------|-------------|
| "Anti-Relleno: Odias la paja" | Anti-filler: Hate padding | Every sentence must add value. No fluff. |
| "Profundidad sobre Amplitud" | Depth over breadth | Take one idea and drill deep, don't list. |
| "La Verdad es Silenciosa" | Truth is quiet | Impact comes from precision, not volume. |
| "Autoridad Tranquila" | Calm authority | Write with confidence, not seeking approval. |
| "Vulnerabilidad Pasada (Cicatriz)" | Past vulnerability (scar) | Share lessons learned, not current complaints. |
| "Busca la Paradoja" | Seek the paradox | Truth hides in contradiction. |

### Business Email Style (From Jorge's Call)

Jorge clarified that his **personal writing** (literary, reflective) is different from **business writing**. For outreach emails:

1. **Direct** - Get to the point fast
2. **Precise** - Specific numbers, names, facts
3. **Empathic** - Acknowledge their situation
4. **Human** - Not robotic, not salesy

### Anti-Patterns (What NOT to Do)

From Jorge's writing agent:
- ❌ NO "5 steps to..." lists
- ❌ NO servile assistant language ("Here's your text!")
- ❌ NO LinkedIn/marketing clichés
- ❌ NO self-help platitudes ("You can do it!", "Believe in yourself")

Instead use:
- ✅ Existential language ("build", "accept", "observe")
- ✅ Short sentences with breathing room
- ✅ One clear idea per message

### Applying to Emails

**Before (Too Long, Too Salesy):**
```
Hi Marco,

Congratulations on your amazing funding round! I'm sure you're super excited about the future. I wanted to reach out because I help startups just like yours build amazing development teams quickly and efficiently. Our process is streamlined and we have a proven track record of success. I'd love to schedule a call to discuss how we might be able to help you achieve your goals!

Best,
Jorge
```

**After (Jorge's Style):**
```
Hi Marco,

Saw the €5M news. Congrats - that's a real milestone.

Quick question: dev team sorted, or still figuring that out?

Most founders I talk to post-raise are stuck between "we need to ship" and "hiring takes 6 months."

We helped a similar Series A go from zero to first sprint in 14 days.

Worth a quick chat?

Jorge
```

**What Changed:**
- Cut word count by 50%
- Removed fluff ("amazing", "super excited", "proven track record")
- One idea per paragraph
- Ended with question, not demand
